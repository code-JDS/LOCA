This problem has two parts. First, we need to plot the positions of the three observed hydrogen sources on a top-down map of the galaxy. Second, we need to explain what could be learned by repeating these measurements at different galactic longitudes \(\ell\).

### Part 1: Positions of the Sources

From sub-problem C.4, we determined the distances of the three sources from the galactic center (C) to be:
*   Source 1: \(R_1 = 0.95 R_{\odot}\)
*   Source 2: \(R_2 = 0.78 R_{\odot}\)
*   Source 3: \(R_3 = 0.67 R_{\odot}\)

All these sources were observed along a single line of sight from the Sun (S) at a galactic longitude of \(\ell = 30^{\circ}\). The position of each source must satisfy two geometric conditions simultaneously:
1.  It must lie on a circle of radius \(R_i\) centered at the galactic center C.
2.  It must lie on the line of sight, which is a straight line originating from the Sun S and making an angle \(\ell = 30^{\circ}\) with the line connecting the Sun and the Galactic Center (SC).

For an observation within the Sun's orbit (\(R < R_{\odot}\)) and for \(0^{\circ} < \ell < 90^{\circ}\), a line of sight will generally intersect a given circular orbit at two distinct points. This is a well-known phenomenon in this method called the **distance ambiguity**.

Let's verify this for our sources. The minimum distance of the line of sight from the galactic center C is given by \(d_{\perp} = R_{\odot} \sin(\ell)\). With \(\ell = 30^{\circ}\), this distance is \(d_{\perp} = R_{\odot} \sin(30^{\circ}) = 0.5 R_{\odot}\). Since all three calculated radii (\(R_1, R_2, R_3\)) are greater than \(0.5 R_{\odot}\), the line of sight intersects each of the three orbits at two points.

Therefore, for each observed signal, there are two possible locations for the source. We have a total of 3 sources, leading to 6 possible positions in the galaxy.

### Part 2: Deduction from Repeated Measurements

By repeating these measurements for a wide range of galactic longitudes \(\ell\), we can systematically probe the galaxy's structure in many different directions. Each measurement at a given \(\ell\) provides the radial distances `R` of hydrogen clouds along that specific line of sight.

When we combine the data from all these different lines of sight, we can create a composite map. By plotting all the determined possible locations of hydrogen clouds, we can reconstruct the overall two-dimensional distribution of neutral hydrogen gas in the galactic plane.

This technique is fundamental to radio astronomy because it allows us to map the structure of our own galaxy from our position within it. The hydrogen clouds are not uniformly distributed; they tend to concentrate in regions of higher density. In a spiral galaxy like the Milky Way, these high-density regions are the **spiral arms**.

Therefore, by performing these measurements across various longitudes, we could deduce the large-scale structure of our galaxy, tracing the locations and shapes of its spiral arms. This method was, in fact, how the spiral structure of the Milky Way was first discovered and mapped.
