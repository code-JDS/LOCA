### C.3 Analysis of Motion Regimes

This problem requires us to determine the conditions for two different behaviors of the mass M and to describe the corresponding evolution of its position `x(t)`. The motion is driven by the pressure fluctuations `P_1(t)` and counteracted by a friction force `F_s` and a restoring force due to the displacement `x`.

#### 1. Force Analysis

The net force exerted by the cables on the mass M, `T_{net} = T_R - T_L`, depends on both the pressure perturbation `P_1` and the displacement `x`. Based on the results from C.1 and C.2, and using the approximations `S_t \ll S_b, S_c`, we can write the total net force as the sum of a pressure-dependent term and a position-dependent term:

*   Force due to pressure change `P_1` (at `x=0`): `T_{net, P_1} \approx \frac{2 S_b S_c}{S_b + S_c} P_1`
*   Force due to displacement `x` (at `P_1=0`): `T_{net, x} \approx -\frac{4 \rho g S_b S_c}{S_b + S_c} x`

The total net force from the cables is:
`T_{net}(x, P_1) = T_{net, P_1} + T_{net, x} \approx \frac{2 S_b S_c}{S_b + S_c} (P_1 - 2 \rho g x)`

The mass M will move only if the magnitude of this net force exceeds the static friction force `F_s`. Since the motion is slow (quasi-static), when the mass is moving, the net force from the cables is balanced by the kinetic friction force.
*   Moving to the right (`dx/dt > 0`): `T_{net}(x, P_1) = F_s`
*   Moving to the left (`dx/dt < 0`): `T_{net}(x, P_1) = -F_s`

#### 2. Equation of Motion in Dimensionless Form

Let's express the equations of motion using the dimensionless parameters `\xi` and `\lambda`:
`\xi \simeq \frac{S_b+S_c}{S_b S_c} \frac{F_s}{A}` and `\lambda \simeq \frac{2 \rho g X}{A}`.

From the definition of `\xi`, we can write `F_s \simeq A \xi \frac{S_b S_c}{S_b+S_c}`.

Substituting this into the equation for motion to the right:
`\frac{2 S_b S_c}{S_b + S_c} (P_1 - 2 \rho g x) = A \xi \frac{S_b S_c}{S_b+S_c}`
`2(P_1 - 2 \rho g x) = A \xi`
`P_1 - 2 \rho g x = \frac{A \xi}{2}`

Solving for `x` and normalizing by `X`:
`x = \frac{1}{2\rho g} \left( P_1 - \frac{A\xi}{2} \right)`
`\frac{x}{X} = \frac{A}{2\rho g X} \left( \frac{P_1}{A} - \frac{\xi}{2} \right)`

Using the definition of `\lambda`, we get the equation for `x/X` when moving to the right:
`\frac{x}{X} = \frac{1}{\lambda} \left( \frac{P_1}{A} - \frac{\xi}{2} \right)` (for `dx/dt > 0`)

Similarly, for motion to the left (`T_{net} = -F_s`):
`\frac{x}{X} = \frac{1}{\lambda} \left( \frac{P_1}{A} + \frac{\xi}{2} \right)` (for `dx/dt < 0`)

These two equations describe the "slip" phases of the motion on a `(P_1/A, x/X)` diagram.

#### 3. Distinguishing the Regimes

The two regimes are distinguished by whether the mass M reaches the stops at `x = \pm X`. Let's consider the maximum possible displacement `x_{peak}` that would occur if the stops were absent. This happens when `P_1` reaches its maximum value, `A`.
Starting from a state of motion to the right, the position at `P_1 = A` would be:
`\frac{x_{peak}}{X} = \frac{1}{\lambda} \left( 1 - \frac{\xi}{2} \right)`

*   **Regime 1:** The mass does not reach the stops. This occurs if the maximum potential displacement is less than `X`.
    `x_{peak} < X \implies \frac{x_{peak}}{X} < 1`
    `\frac{1}{\lambda} \left( 1 - \frac{\xi}{2} \right) < 1 \implies 1 - \frac{\xi}{2} < \lambda`
    Condition: `\lambda > 1 - \xi/2`

*   **Regime 2:** The mass reaches the stops. This occurs if the maximum potential displacement is greater than or equal to `X`.
    `x_{peak} \ge X \implies \frac{x_{peak}}{X} \ge 1`
    `\frac{1}{\lambda} \left( 1 - \frac{\xi}{2} \right) \ge 1 \implies 1 - \frac{\xi}{2} \ge \lambda`
    Condition: `\lambda \le 1 - \xi/2`

#### 4. Sketching the Motion `x(t)/X`

We now describe the steady-state motion for each regime, which is established after an initial transient. The motion of `x(t)` is periodic with the same period `\tau_1` as `P_1(t)`.

**Regime 1: `\lambda > 1 - \xi/2` (No contact with stops)**

The mass oscillates between a maximum position `x_{max} = X(1/\lambda)(1-\xi/2)` and a minimum position `x_{min} = -x_{max}`. The motion is characterized by "stick-slip" behavior.
*   **Slip (moving):** When the magnitude of the driving force `|T_{net}|` is equal to `F_s`, the mass moves. Since `x` is a linear function of `P_1` and `P_1` is a linear function of `t`, `x` moves linearly with time.
*   **Stick (stationary):** When the mass reverses direction, it must wait for the pressure `P_1` to change enough to overcome the static friction in the opposite direction. During this time, `x` remains constant. This happens when `P_1` is near its peak (`A`) and trough (`-A`).

The graph of `x(t)/X` is a periodic wave with an amplitude of `(1/\lambda)(1-\xi/2)`, which is less than 1. Each cycle consists of four segments:
1.  A linear increase in `x`.
2.  A flat plateau at `x_{max}/X`.
3.  A linear decrease in `x`.
4.  A flat plateau at `x_{min}/X`.

**Regime 2: `\lambda \le 1 - \xi/2` (Contact with stops)**

The mass hits the stops at `x=X` and `x=-X`.
*   The mass moves from one stop to the other when the pressure conditions are met. For example, it moves from `x=-X` to `x=X` when `P_1/A` sweeps through the range `[\xi/2 - \lambda, \xi/2 + \lambda]`.
*   The mass then remains stationary at the stop (`x=X` or `x=-X`) for a significant portion of the cycle, waiting for the pressure to change enough to initiate movement in the opposite direction.

The graph of `x(t)/X` is a trapezoidal wave with an amplitude of exactly 1. Each cycle consists of four segments:
1.  A linear increase from -1 to +1.
2.  A long flat plateau at +1.
3.  A linear decrease from +1 to -1.
4.  A long flat plateau at -1.
