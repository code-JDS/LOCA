### Analysis of `m_add` as a function of `h_t`

The problem asks for the evolution of the additional mass, `m_add`, as the two-part barometric tube is lifted. The position of the tube is given by `h_t`, the altitude of the junction between the tube and the bulb, which varies in the range `h_t \in [-H_b, H_t]`. The additional mass `m_add` is defined by the equilibrium pulling force `F = (m_{tb} + m_{add})g`.

To find `m_add`, we first express the pulling force `F`. We consider the system composed of the tube itself (`m_{tb}`) and the mercury column contained within it (`m_{merc}`). The vertical forces acting on this combined system are:
1.  The upward pulling force `F`.
2.  The downward gravitational force on the tube, `m_{tb}g`.
3.  The downward gravitational force on the mercury inside, `m_{merc}g`.
4.  The upward pressure force from the external mercury bath on the open bottom of the tube. The bottom is at altitude `z_{base} = h_t - H_t`. Since `h_t \le H_t`, `z_{base} \le 0`, so the opening is always submerged. The pressure at this depth is `P_{ext}(z_{base}) = P_a - \rho g z_{base} = P_a - \rho g (h_t - H_t)`. The upward force is `F_p = P_{ext}(z_{base}) S_t`.

At equilibrium, the net force is zero:
`F - m_{tb}g - m_{merc}g + [P_a - \rho g (h_t - H_t)]S_t = 0`
`F = m_{tb}g + m_{merc}g - P_a S_t + \rho g (h_t - H_t)S_t`

Comparing this with `F = (m_{tb} + m_{add})g`, we get:
`m_{add}g = m_{merc}g - P_a S_t + \rho g (h_t - H_t)S_t`
`m_{add} = m_{merc} - \frac{P_a S_t}{g} + \rho (h_t - H_t)S_t`

Here, `m_{merc} = \rho V_{merc}`, where `V_{merc}` is the volume of mercury inside the tube. This volume depends on the height of the mercury column, `z_l`. The height `z_l` is determined by hydrostatic equilibrium. The pressure at the top of the mercury column is the pressure of the vacuum, `P_{sat} \approx 0`. The pressure at the level of the external bath (`z=0`) inside the tube must equal the atmospheric pressure `P_a`.
`P_{in}(0) = P_{top} + \rho g (z_l - 0) = \rho g z_l`
`P_{in}(0) = P_a`
This gives `z_l = P_a / (\rho g)`. Let's call this constant barometric height `h_{baro}`.
`h_{baro} = \frac{P_0}{\rho g} = \frac{1.000 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}}{13.5 \times 10^3 \text{ kg/m}^3 \times 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2} \approx 0.756 \text{ m} = 75.6 \text{ cm}`.

This holds as long as a vacuum can form, i.e., the top of the tube `z_{top} = h_t + H_b` is above `z_l = h_{baro}`. If `h_t + H_b \le h_{baro}`, the mercury fills the tube to the top, so `z_l = h_t + H_b`.

We can distinguish three regimes for `h_t` in the range `[-H_b, H_t] = [-20 \text{ cm}, 80 \text{ cm}]`:

**Regime 1: `h_t \in [-H_b, h_{baro} - H_b]`**
In this range, `h_t + H_b \le h_{baro}`. The mercury fills the entire tube volume. There is no vacuum.
-   `z_l = h_t + H_b`.
-   The volume of mercury `V_{merc}` is the total internal volume of the tube: `V_{merc} = S_t H_t + S_b H_b`.
-   The mass `m_{merc}` is constant: `m_{merc} = \rho(S_t H_t + S_b H_b)`.
-   The additional mass is:
    `m_{add} = \rho(S_t H_t + S_b H_b) - \frac{P_0 S_t}{g} + \rho(h_t - H_t)S_t`
    `m_{add} = \rho S_b H_b - \frac{P_0 S_t}{g} + \rho S_t h_t`
-   This is a linear function of `h_t`. The slope is `\frac{dm_{add}}{dh_t} = \rho S_t`.

**Regime 2: `h_t \in [h_{baro} - H_b, h_{baro}]`**
Here, `h_t + H_b > h_{baro}`, so a vacuum forms and `z_l = h_{baro}`. Also, `h_t \le h_{baro}`, so the mercury surface is within the bulb.
-   `z_l = h_{baro} = P_0/(\rho g)`.
-   The volume of mercury `V_{merc}` fills the narrow tube up to `h_t` and the bulb up to `z_l`:
    `V_{merc} = S_t H_t + S_b(z_l - h_t) = S_t H_t + S_b(\frac{P_0}{\rho g} - h_t)`.
-   The additional mass is:
    `m_{add} = \rho \left( S_t H_t + S_b(\frac{P_0}{\rho g} - h_t) \right) - \frac{P_0 S_t}{g} + \rho(h_t - H_t)S_t`
    `m_{add} = \rho S_t H_t + \frac{S_b P_0}{g} - \rho S_b h_t - \frac{P_0 S_t}{g} + \rho S_t h_t - \rho S_t H_t`
    `m_{add} = \frac{(S_b - S_t)P_0}{g} - \rho(S_b - S_t)h_t = \rho(S_b - S_t)\left(\frac{P_0}{\rho g} - h_t\right)`
-   This is a linear function of `h_t`. The slope is `\frac{dm_{add}}{dh_t} = -\rho(S_b - S_t)`.

**Regime 3: `h_t \in [h_{baro}, H_t]`**
Here, `h_t > h_{baro}`, so the mercury surface `z_l = h_{baro}` is within the narrow tube part.
-   `z_l = h_{baro} = P_0/(\rho g)`.
-   The volume of mercury `V_{merc}` fills the narrow tube from its bottom `h_t - H_t` up to `z_l`:
    `V_{merc} = S_t(z_l - (h_t - H_t)) = S_t(\frac{P_0}{\rho g} - h_t + H_t)`.
-   The additional mass is:
    `m_{add} = \rho S_t(\frac{P_0}{\rho g} - h_t + H_t) - \frac{P_0 S_t}{g} + \rho(h_t - H_t)S_t`
    `m_{add} = \frac{S_t P_0}{g} - \rho S_t h_t + \rho S_t H_t - \frac{P_0 S_t}{g} + \rho S_t h_t - \rho S_t H_t`
    `m_{add} = 0`
-   The additional mass is constant and equal to zero. The slope is `\frac{dm_{add}}{dh_t} = 0`.

**Summary and Sketch**

The evolution of `m_{add}` is piecewise linear. The angular points (where the slope changes) occur at:
1.  `h_{t,1} = h_{baro} - H_b = \frac{P_0}{\rho g} - H_b`
2.  `h_{t,2} = h_{baro} = \frac{P_0}{\rho g}`

The slopes of the segments are:
-   For `h_t \in [-H_b, \frac{P_0}{\rho g} - H_b]`: Slope = `\rho S_t`
-   For `h_t \in [\frac{P_0}{\rho g} - H_b, \frac{P_0}{\rho g}]`: Slope = `- \rho(S_b - S_t)`
-   For `h_t \in [\frac{P_0}{\rho g}, H_t]`: Slope = `0`
