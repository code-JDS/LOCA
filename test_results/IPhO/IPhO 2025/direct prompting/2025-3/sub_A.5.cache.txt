### A.5. Bubble radius upon reaching the surface

The goal is to determine the radius \(a_{H_{\ell}}\) of a bubble when it reaches the free surface of the champagne, after rising a height \(H_{\ell}\). The bubble grows as it rises.

**1. Formulation of the physical model**

We need to relate the bubble's vertical motion to its growth.
Let \(z(t)\) be the vertical position of the bubble, measured upwards from the bottom of the glass. The bubble's velocity is \(v = \frac{dz}{dt}\).
The problem states that the bubble is always at its terminal velocity. From the solution to sub-problem A.4, the terminal velocity \(v\) of a bubble of radius \(a\) is given by:
\[ v(a) = \frac{2 a^2 g_0 (\rho_{\ell} - \rho_{gas})}{9 \eta} \]
Since the density of the gas (\(\rho_{gas} \approx 1.8 \, \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^{-3}\)) is much smaller than the density of the liquid (\(\rho_{\ell} = 1.0 \times 10^3 \, \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^{-3}\)), we can approximate this as:
\[ v(a) \approx \frac{2 g_0 \rho_{\ell}}{9 \eta} a^2 = C a^2 \]
where \(C = \frac{2 g_0 \rho_{\ell}}{9 \eta}\) is a constant.

The bubble also grows while rising. The problem states that the growth rate \(q_a = \frac{da}{dt}\) is constant. This gives a relationship between time \(t\) and radius \(a\):
\[ \frac{da}{dt} = q_a \implies dt = \frac{da}{q_a} \]

**2. Derivation of the final radius**

We can express the total height travelled, \(H_{\ell}\), by integrating the velocity over the time it takes to rise, \(t_{rise}\):
\[ H_{\ell} = \int_0^{H_{\ell}} dz = \int_0^{t_{rise}} v(t) dt \]
To solve this integral, we change the integration variable from time \(t\) to radius \(a\). The radius changes from its initial value \(a_0\) at \(t=0\) to its final value \(a_{H_{\ell}}\) at \(t=t_{rise}\).
Using \(dt = \frac{da}{q_a}\) and \(v(t) = v(a(t)) = C a^2\), the integral becomes:
\[ H_{\ell} = \int_{a_0}^{a_{H_{\ell}}} v(a) \frac{dt}{da} da = \int_{a_0}^{a_{H_{\ell}}} (C a^2) \frac{1}{q_a} da \]
\[ H_{\ell} = \frac{C}{q_a} \int_{a_0}^{a_{H_{\ell}}} a^2 da \]
Evaluating the integral gives:
\[ H_{\ell} = \frac{C}{q_a} \left[ \frac{a^3}{3} \right]_{a_0}^{a_{H_{\ell}}} = \frac{C}{3q_a} (a_{H_{\ell}}^3 - a_0^3) \]
The problem states we can assume \(a_{H_{\ell}} \gg a_0\). This implies \(a_{H_{\ell}}^3 \gg a_0^3\), so we can neglect the \(a_0^3\) term:
\[ H_{\ell} \approx \frac{C}{3q_a} a_{H_{\ell}}^3 \]
Now, we can solve for the final radius \(a_{H_{\ell}}\):
\[ a_{H_{\ell}}^3 \approx \frac{3 q_a H_{\ell}}{C} \]
Substituting the expression for the constant \(C = \frac{2 g_0 \rho_{\ell}}{9 \eta}\):
\[ a_{H_{\ell}}^3 \approx \frac{3 q_a H_{\ell}}{\frac{2 g_0 \rho_{\ell}}{9 \eta}} = \frac{27 q_a H_{\ell} \eta}{2 g_0 \rho_{\ell}} \]
Taking the cube root gives the final symbolic expression for the radius:
\[ a_{H_{\ell}} = \left( \frac{27 q_a H_{\ell} \eta}{2 g_0 \rho_{\ell}} \right)^{1/3} \]

**3. Numerical Calculation**

We are asked to calculate the numerical value of \(a_{H_{\ell}}\) (denoted as \(a_{H_t}\) in the problem, which is likely a typo) for a height \(H_{\ell} = 10 \text{ cm}\) and with the growth rate \(q_a\) from Fig. 2.

The required values are:
- Growth rate from Fig. 2 (and A.3): \(q_a = 0.23 \, \text{mm/s} = 2.3 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m/s}\)
- Height of liquid: \(H_{\ell} = 10 \, \text{cm} = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- Dynamic viscosity (from A.4): \(\eta \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa} \cdot \text{s}\)
- Density of liquid: \(\rho_{\ell} = 1.0 \times 10^3 \, \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^{-3}\)
- Acceleration due to gravity (standard value): \(g_0 \approx 9.8 \, \text{m} \cdot \text{s}^{-2}\)

Substituting these values into our expression for \(a_{H_{\ell}}\):
\[ a_{H_{\ell}} = \left( \frac{27 \times (2.3 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m/s}) \times (0.1 \, \text{m}) \times (1.7 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa} \cdot \text{s})}{2 \times (9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2) \times (1.0 \times 10^3 \, \text{kg/m}^3)} \right)^{1/3} \]
\[ a_{H_{\ell}} = \left( \frac{1.0539 \times 10^{-6}}{1.96 \times 10^4} \right)^{1/3} \, \text{m} \]
\[ a_{H_{\ell}} = (5.377 \times 10^{-11})^{1/3} \, \text{m} \]
To compute the cube root, we can write this as:
\[ a_{H_{\ell}} = (53.77 \times 10^{-12})^{1/3} \, \text{m} = (53.77)^{1/3} \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m} \]
Since \(3^3 = 27\) and \(4^3 = 64\), the cube root of 53.77 is between 3 and 4. A more precise calculation gives \((53.77)^{1/3} \approx 3.775\).
\[ a_{H_{\ell}} \approx 3.775 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m} \]
Rounding to two significant figures, consistent with the input data:
\[ a_{H_{\ell}} \approx 3.8 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m} = 0.38 \, \text{mm} \]

### Final Answer
\[ \boxed{ a_{H_{\ell}} = \left( \frac{27 q_a H_{\ell} \eta}{2 g_0 \rho_{\ell}} \right)^{1/3}, \quad a_{H_{\ell}} \approx 3.8 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m} } \]
