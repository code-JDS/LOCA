To determine the maximum height \(H_c\) reached by the cork stopper, we will follow a multi-step process based on the work-energy theorem. The cork is accelerated along the length of the bottleneck, \(\ell_0\), by the forces acting on it. The net work done on the cork during this phase is converted into kinetic energy. This kinetic energy is then converted into potential energy as the cork rises against gravity.

The steps are as follows:
1.  Determine the friction force acting on the cork.
2.  Calculate the net work done on the cork as it moves a distance \(\ell_0\) and exits the bottleneck.
3.  Use the work-energy theorem to find the launch velocity of the cork.
4.  Calculate the maximum height reached using the launch velocity.

**1. Determining the Friction Force**

The problem states that "Initially, the pressure force slightly overcomes the friction force." This implies that at the moment the cork begins to move, the forces are nearly balanced. We can model this by setting the total upward force equal to the total downward force.

The forces acting on the cork at the initial position (\(x=0\)) are:
-   **Upward pressure force:** \(F_{gas} = P_i S_c\), where \(S_c = \pi (d/2)^2\) is the cross-sectional area of the cork.
-   **Downward atmospheric pressure force:** \(F_{atm} = P_0 S_c\).
-   **Downward friction force:** \(F_{friction, initial} = \alpha A_0\), where \(A_0 = \pi d \ell_0\) is the initial contact area.
-   **Downward gravitational force:** \(F_{g} = mg_0\).

The equilibrium condition is:
\(F_{gas} = F_{atm} + F_{friction, initial} + F_{g}\)
\((P_i - P_0)S_c = F_{friction, initial} + mg_0\)

From this, we can find the initial friction force, which we will need to calculate the work done by friction.
\(F_{friction, initial} = (P_i - P_0)S_c - mg_0\)

Let's calculate the numerical values:
-   Cork mass \(m = 10 \text{ g} = 0.01 \text{ kg}\)
-   Cork diameter \(d = 1.8 \text{ cm} = 0.018 \text{ m}\)
-   Cork length in bottleneck \(\ell_0 = 2.5 \text{ cm} = 0.025 \text{ m}\)
-   Initial pressure at \(T_0=6^{\circ} C\): \(P_i = 4.69 \text{ bar} = 4.69 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}\)
-   Atmospheric pressure \(P_0 = 1.0 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}\)
-   Gravitational acceleration \(g_0 \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\)

Cork area: \(S_c = \pi (0.018/2)^2 = \pi (0.009)^2 \approx 2.545 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2\).
Initial friction force:
\(F_{friction, initial} = (4.69 \times 10^5 - 1.0 \times 10^5) \times (2.545 \times 10^{-4}) - (0.01 \times 9.8)\)
\(F_{friction, initial} = (3.69 \times 10^5) \times (2.545 \times 10^{-4}) - 0.098 \approx 93.91 - 0.098 = 93.81 \text{ N}\).

**2. Calculating the Net Work Done**

The net work done on the cork, \(W_{net}\), is the integral of the net force over the distance \(\ell_0\).
\(W_{net} = W_{gas} - W_{atm} - W_{friction} - W_{gravity}\)

-   **Work done by the expanding gas (\(W_{gas}\)):**
    The gas expands adiabatically. The work done is \(W_{gas} = \frac{P_i V_G - P_f V_f}{\gamma - 1}\).
    Initial gas volume \(V_G = 25 \text{ ml} = 25 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^3\).
    Final volume \(V_f = V_G + S_c \ell_0 = 25 \times 10^{-6} + (2.545 \times 10^{-4})(0.025) = 25 \times 10^{-6} + 6.36 \times 10^{-6} = 31.36 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^3\).
    Final pressure \(P_f = P_i (V_G/V_f)^\gamma = (4.69 \times 10^5) \left(\frac{25}{31.36}\right)^{1.3} \approx 3.48 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}\).
    \(W_{gas} = \frac{(4.69 \times 10^5)(25 \times 10^{-6}) - (3.48 \times 10^5)(31.36 \times 10^{-6})}{1.3 - 1}\)
    \(W_{gas} = \frac{11.725 - 10.91}{0.3} = \frac{0.815}{0.3} \approx 2.72 \text{ J}\).

-   **Work done against atmospheric pressure (\(W_{atm}\)):**
    \(W_{atm} = P_0 S_c \ell_0 = (1.0 \times 10^5)(2.545 \times 10^{-4})(0.025) \approx 0.64 \text{ J}\).

-   **Work done against friction (\(W_{friction}\)):**
    The friction force is \(F_{friction}(x) = \alpha A(x) = \alpha \pi d (\ell_0 - x)\). It decreases linearly from \(F_{friction, initial}\) at \(x=0\) to 0 at \(x=\ell_0\). The work done is the area of a triangle:
    \(W_{friction} = \frac{1}{2} F_{friction, initial} \ell_0 = \frac{1}{2} (93.81 \text{ N})(0.025 \text{ m}) \approx 1.17 \text{ J}\).

-   **Work done against gravity (\(W_{gravity}\)):**
    \(W_{gravity} = mg_0 \ell_0 = (0.01 \text{ kg})(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(0.025 \text{ m}) = 0.00245 \text{ J}\).

-   **Net Work:**
    \(W_{net} = 2.72 - 0.64 - 1.17 - 0.00245 \approx 0.908 \text{ J}\).

**3. & 4. Calculating the Maximum Height**

The net work done on the cork is equal to its kinetic energy upon leaving the bottleneck:
\(KE_{launch} = W_{net} = \frac{1}{2} m v_{launch}^2 \approx 0.908 \text{ J}\).

After leaving the bottleneck, the cork travels upwards, and its kinetic energy is converted into gravitational potential energy. The problem states to neglect the net pressure force after the cork leaves the bottleneck. We also neglect air resistance. The maximum height \(H_c\) (measured from the launch point) is reached when all kinetic energy is converted to potential energy:
\(mg_0 H_c = KE_{launch} = W_{net}\)
\(H_c = \frac{W_{net}}{mg_0}\)
\(H_c = \frac{0.908 \text{ J}}{(0.01 \text{ kg})(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)} = \frac{0.908}{0.098} \approx 9.26 \text{ m}\).

Rounding to two significant figures, consistent with the input data:
\(H_c \approx 9.3 \text{ m}\).
