### Part B. Acoustic emission of a bursting bubble

When the film bursts, it releases internal pressure and emits a sound. We model this acoustic emission by a Helmholtz resonator: a cavity open to the atmosphere at \(P_{0}\) through a bottleneck aperture of area \(S\) (Fig. 5. left). In the neck, a mass \(m_{p}\) makes small amplitude position oscillations due to the pressure forces it experiences as the gas in the cavity expands or compresses adiabatically. The gravity force on \(m_{p}\) is negligible compared to pressure forces. Let \(V_{0}\) be the volume of gas under the mass \(m_{p}\) for \(P=P_{0}\) as \(z=0\).

To find the frequency of oscillation \(f_0\), we will model the system as a simple harmonic oscillator. We will derive the equation of motion for the mass \(m_p\) and identify the angular frequency from it.

**1. Equation of Motion**
Let \(z\) be the small vertical displacement of the mass \(m_p\) from its equilibrium position, with the positive direction pointing outwards from the cavity. According to Newton's second law, the equation of motion for the mass is:
\[ m_p \frac{d^2z}{dt^2} = F_{net} \]
where \(F_{net}\) is the net force acting on the mass.

**2. Net Force Calculation**
The net force is due to the pressure difference between the gas inside the cavity (\(P\)) and the atmosphere outside (\(P_0\)). The area of the aperture (piston) is \(S\).
\[ F_{net} = (P - P_0)S \]
At equilibrium (\(z=0\)), the pressure inside is \(P_0\) and the volume is \(V_0\). When the mass is displaced by \(z\), the volume of the gas in the cavity changes to:
\[ V = V_0 + Sz \]

**3. Adiabatic Process**
The problem states that the gas in the cavity expands and compresses adiabatically. The relationship between pressure and volume for an adiabatic process is given by:
\[ PV^\gamma = \text{constant} = P_0 V_0^\gamma \]
We can express the pressure \(P\) inside the cavity as a function of the displacement \(z\):
\[ P = P_0 \left(\frac{V_0}{V}\right)^\gamma = P_0 \left(\frac{V_0}{V_0 + Sz}\right)^\gamma = P_0 \left(1 + \frac{Sz}{V_0}\right)^{-\gamma} \]

**4. Linearization for Small Oscillations**
For small amplitude oscillations, the displacement \(z\) is small, which means the term \(\varepsilon = \frac{Sz}{V_0}\) is much less than 1. We can use the provided hint, the binomial approximation \((1+\varepsilon)^\alpha \approx 1 + \alpha\varepsilon\), with \(\alpha = -\gamma\).
\[ P \approx P_0 \left(1 - \gamma \frac{Sz}{V_0}\right) \]
Now, we can find the net force by substituting this linearized pressure into the force equation:
\[ F_{net} = (P - P_0)S \approx \left[ P_0 \left(1 - \gamma \frac{Sz}{V_0}\right) - P_0 \right] S \]
\[ F_{net} \approx \left( P_0 - \gamma \frac{P_0 Sz}{V_0} - P_0 \right) S \]
\[ F_{net} \approx - \left(\frac{\gamma P_0 S^2}{V_0}\right) z \]
This is a linear restoring force of the form \(F = -kz\), where the effective spring constant is \(k = \frac{\gamma P_0 S^2}{V_0}\).

**5. Frequency of Oscillation**
Substituting the restoring force into Newton's second law gives the equation for a simple harmonic oscillator:
\[ m_p \frac{d^2z}{dt^2} = - \left(\frac{\gamma P_0 S^2}{V_0}\right) z \]
\[ \frac{d^2z}{dt^2} + \left(\frac{\gamma P_0 S^2}{m_p V_0}\right) z = 0 \]
This equation is of the standard form \(\frac{d^2z}{dt^2} + \omega_0^2 z = 0\), where \(\omega_0\) is the angular frequency of oscillation. By comparison, we have:
\[ \omega_0^2 = \frac{\gamma P_0 S^2}{m_p V_0} \]
The angular frequency is:
\[ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_0 S^2}{m_p V_0}} = S \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_0}{m_p V_0}} \]
The frequency of oscillation \(f_0\) is related to the angular frequency by \(f_0 = \frac{\omega_0}{2\pi}\). Therefore, the frequency of the Helmholtz resonator is:
\[ f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi} S \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_0}{m_p V_0}} \]

### Final Answer
\[ \boxed{ f_{0} = \frac{S}{2\pi} \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_{0}}{m_{p} V_{0}}} } \]
