### Part A. Nucleation, growth and rise of bubbles

#### A.1 Express the pressure \(P_{b}\) in terms of \(P_{0}\), \(a\) and \(\sigma\).

The pressure difference between the inside and the outside of a spherical bubble is due to the surface tension of the liquid. This relationship is described by the Young-Laplace equation.

Let's consider a spherical bubble of radius \(a\) submerged in a liquid.
- The pressure inside the bubble is \(P_{b}\).
- The pressure in the liquid just outside the bubble is \(P_{0}\).
- The surface tension of the liquid is \(\sigma\).

To derive the relationship, we can consider the equilibrium of one hemisphere of the bubble. The forces acting on the equatorial plane of this hemisphere are:
1.  **Force due to internal pressure:** The pressure \(P_{b}\) acts outwards on the circular cross-sectional area \(\pi a^2\). The force is \(F_{int} = P_{b} \cdot \pi a^2\).
2.  **Force due to external pressure:** The pressure \(P_{0}\) acts inwards on the same cross-sectional area. The force is \(F_{ext} = P_{0} \cdot \pi a^2\).
3.  **Force due to surface tension:** The surface tension \(\sigma\) acts along the circumference of the circle, \(2\pi a\), pulling the hemisphere inwards to minimize the surface area. The force is \(F_{\sigma} = \sigma \cdot 2\pi a\).

For the hemisphere to be in mechanical equilibrium, the outward force must balance the inward forces:
\[ F_{int} = F_{ext} + F_{\sigma} \]
\[ P_{b} \cdot \pi a^2 = P_{0} \cdot \pi a^2 + \sigma \cdot 2\pi a \]

Now, we can solve for the pressure difference \((P_{b} - P_{0})\) by dividing the entire equation by \(\pi a^2\):
\[ P_{b} = P_{0} + \frac{2\sigma \cdot \pi a}{\pi a^2} \]
\[ P_{b} - P_{0} = \frac{2\sigma}{a} \]

This is the Young-Laplace equation for a spherical bubble with a single interface. The question asks for the expression of the pressure \(P_{b}\). Rearranging the equation, we get:
\[ P_{b} = P_{0} + \frac{2\sigma}{a} \]

This equation shows that the pressure inside the bubble, \(P_{b}\), is greater than the surrounding liquid pressure, \(P_{0}\), by an amount \(2\sigma/a\), which is known as the Laplace pressure.

### Final Answer
\[ \boxed{P_{b} = P_{0} + \frac{2\sigma}{a}} \]
