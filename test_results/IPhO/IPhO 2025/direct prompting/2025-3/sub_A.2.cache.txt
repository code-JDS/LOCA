### A.2 Express the critical radius \(a_{c}\) above which a bubble is expected to grow in terms of \(P_{0}\), \(\sigma\), \(c_{\ell}\) and \(c_{0}\) where \(c_{0}=k_{H} P_{0}\) .Calculate numerically \(a_{c}\) for \(c_{\ell}=4 c_{0}\) (0.5 pt)

A bubble of carbon dioxide will grow if there is a net diffusion of dissolved \(\text{CO}_{2}\) from the liquid into the bubble. This occurs when the concentration of dissolved \(\text{CO}_{2}\) in the bulk liquid, \(c_{\ell}\), is greater than the concentration of dissolved \(\text{CO}_{2}\) at the bubble's surface, \(c_{b}\).

Growth condition: \(c_{\ell} > c_{b}\)
Shrinking condition: \(c_{\ell} < c_{b}\)

The critical radius, \(a_c\), corresponds to the equilibrium state where the bubble neither grows nor shrinks. This happens when the concentrations are equal:
\[c_{\ell} = c_{b}\]

The concentration at the bubble surface, \(c_b\), is in equilibrium with the pressure inside the bubble, \(P_b\), according to Henry's law:
\[c_{b} = k_{H} P_{b}\]

From the previous sub-problem (A.1), we have the expression for the pressure inside the bubble from the Young-Laplace equation:
\[P_{b} = P_{0} + \frac{2\sigma}{a}\]
where \(P_0\) is the ambient pressure in the liquid, \(\sigma\) is the surface tension, and \(a\) is the bubble radius.

Substituting the expression for \(P_b\) into Henry's law, we get the concentration at the surface as a function of the radius \(a\):
\[c_{b}(a) = k_{H} \left( P_{0} + \frac{2\sigma}{a} \right)\]
Using the given definition \(c_{0} = k_{H} P_{0}\), we can rewrite this as:
\[c_{b}(a) = k_{H} P_{0} + \frac{2\sigma k_{H}}{a} = c_{0} + \frac{2\sigma k_{H}}{a}\]

At the critical radius \(a_c\), we have \(c_{\ell} = c_{b}(a_c)\). Therefore:
\[c_{\ell} = c_{0} + \frac{2\sigma k_{H}}{a_{c}}\]

We can now solve for the critical radius \(a_c\):
\[c_{\ell} - c_{0} = \frac{2\sigma k_{H}}{a_{c}}\]
\[a_{c} = \frac{2\sigma k_{H}}{c_{\ell} - c_{0}}\]

The question asks for the expression in terms of \(P_{0}\), \(\sigma\), \(c_{\ell}\), and \(c_{0}\). We can eliminate Henry's constant \(k_H\) by using the relation \(c_{0} = k_{H} P_{0}\), which gives \(k_{H} = c_{0}/P_{0}\). Substituting this into the expression for \(a_c\):
\[a_{c} = \frac{2\sigma (c_{0}/P_{0})}{c_{\ell} - c_{0}} = \frac{2\sigma c_{0}}{P_{0}(c_{\ell} - c_{0})}\]
This is the expression for the critical radius in the requested terms.

**Numerical Calculation**

We are asked to calculate \(a_c\) for the specific case where the concentration in the liquid is \(c_{\ell} = 4c_{0}\). We can use the simplified expression for \(a_c\) derived from the general formula:
\[a_{c} = \frac{2\sigma c_{0}}{P_{0}(4c_{0} - c_{0})} = \frac{2\sigma c_{0}}{P_{0}(3c_{0})} = \frac{2\sigma}{3P_{0}}\]

Now, we plug in the given numerical values:
- Surface tension \(\sigma = 47 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{m}^{-2}\)
- Atmospheric pressure \(P_{0} = 1.0 \times 10^{5} \, \text{Pa}\)

\[a_{c} = \frac{2 \times (47 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{m}^{-2})}{3 \times (1.0 \times 10^{5} \, \text{Pa})} = \frac{94 \times 10^{-3}}{3.0 \times 10^{5}} \, \text{m}\]
\[a_{c} = \frac{94}{3.0} \times 10^{-8} \, \text{m} \approx 31.33 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{m}\]

Rounding to two significant figures, consistent with the given data:
\[a_{c} \approx 31 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{m} = 3.1 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{m} = 0.31 \, \mu\text{m}\]

### Final Answer
The expression for the critical radius \(a_c\) is:
\[ a_{c} = \frac{2\sigma c_{0}}{P_{0}(c_{\ell} - c_{0})} \]
For \(c_{\ell} = 4c_{0}\), the numerical value of the critical radius is:
\[ \boxed{a_c = \frac{2\sigma}{3P_0} \approx 3.1 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{m}} \]
