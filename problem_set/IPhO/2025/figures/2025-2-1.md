### Fig. 1

This figure consists of three panels illustrating the process of lifting a tube out of the water.

In panel (a), the top (closed end) of the tube is flush with the water surface (containing no air inside, i.e., $h=z_l=0$, where $h$ denotes the height of the tube's top and $z_l$ denotes the height of the water surface inside the tube, with the external water level defined as 0). The total length of the tube is $H$, and the gravitational acceleration is $g$.

Panel (b) depicts the case where the tube is lifted by a certain distance while the interior remains completely filled with water; that is, $h=z_l$ still holds.

Panel (c) shows the tube lifted higher than in panel (b) (while the bottom (open end) of the tube remains submerged), at which point $h > z_l$.

These two figures are unnumbered; they correspond to the two possible variations in behavior during the tube-lifting process described in the preamble to Question A.2.

Behavior A spans from $h=0$ to $h=H$, representing the transition from the top of the tube being flush with the water surface to the bottom of the tube being just about to detach from the liquid surface. The applied force $F$ varies linearly with $h$ throughout this process, ranging from $F=F_0$ at $h=0$ to $F=F_{\max}$ at $h=H$.

Behavior B also spans from $h=0$ to $h=H$. The variation of the applied force $F$ starts from $F=F_0$ and initially increases linearly with $h$ (for $0 < h < h^*$). When $h^* < h$, the force $F$ remains constant (maintained at $F_{\max}$).
