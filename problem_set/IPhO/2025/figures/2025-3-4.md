### Fig. 4

This figure illustrates a model of the bursting process of a bubble after it rises to the liquid surface.

The left panel displays the rupture process of a bubble with its top portion protruding from the liquid surface, where the bubble radius is $a$, the cross-sectional radius of the exposed liquid film is $r_c$, the internal gas pressure is $P_b$, the atmospheric pressure is $P_0$, and the film thickness is $h$.

The upper part of the right panel depicts the retraction state of the liquid film at time $t$ after the rupture. At $t=0$, the bubble begins to rupture from the center of the liquid film directly at the apex (Point A), forming an expanding circular hole with a radius denoted as $r$. Assuming the tangential retraction velocity of the hole's edge remains constant at $v_f$, the radius of the hole expands to $r(t)$ at time $t$.

The lower part of the right panel shows a cross-section of the retracting film. It establishes a coordinate system with the vertical axis $z$ and the origin at the center of the rupture (Point $A$), illustrating that a toroidal rim of accumulated liquid forms at the edge of the retracting film (the radius of this ring corresponds to $r(t)$). The cross-section of this rim can be modeled as a circle with radius $R_m$, and the film retraction velocity $v_f$ corresponds to the moving velocity of the center of this circle. The dotted rectangular region to the right of the rim represents the specific segment of the undisturbed liquid film that is being absorbed. This segment has a thickness $h$ and a horizontal length equal to the distance the rim travels, $v_f \mathrm{d}t$. As the rim moves outward with velocity $\vec{v}_f$, this dotted volume of liquid is swept up and assimilated into the toroidal rim, causing the rim's mass (and potentially its radius $R_m$) to increase over time.
