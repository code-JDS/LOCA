### Fig. 2

This figure illustrates the general layout of the measurement scheme. First, point $C$ represents the Galactic Center, and the Sun is located at point $S$, at a radial distance $R_{\odot}$ of $8\,\mathrm{kpc}$ from $C$. A celestial object emitting hydrogen spectral lines is located at point $E$ (in the figure, $E$ lies on a circular orbit with a radius from the Galactic Center smaller than $8\,\mathrm{kpc}$, i.e., $R = CE < CS$). The line connecting $S$ and $E$ is referred to as the line of sight. The angle from $SC$ to $SE$ counterclockwise is $l$. (In the example shown, both the observed object and the Sun are moving in clockwise circular orbits around the Galaxy.) $l$ is an acute angle. The line of observation direction points from $S$ to $E$, and its unit vector is $\hat{u}_v$. The angle $\beta$ between $\vec{v}_E$ and $\hat{u}_v$ is acute. The angle between $\vec{v}_{\odot}$ and $\hat{u}_v$, which is $\pi/2-\alpha$, is also an acute angle.
